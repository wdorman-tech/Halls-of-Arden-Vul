> [!warning] 🏠 **Home Rule Update (2026-05-06):** The encumbrance system described in this original Player Doc is **no longer in use**. The slot grid is kept as an organisational inventory tracker, but there are **no carry-weight penalties** — no STR-row gating, no movement reductions from carried items, no surprise penalties. Item weights in pounds are kept as a descriptive property only. Mules use a 6 × 4 = 24-slot pack-saddle grid. See [[Houserules Index\|Houserules Index]] and [[Slot-Based Inventory\|Slot-Based Inventory]] for the current rule. The encumbrance section below is retained as historical context.

The system we will be using is OSRIC (Old School Reference and Index Compilation) 3rd Edition. The rules are available [here](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/550861/osric-3-0-player-guide?src=hottest_filtered), you can download them for free by pressing the zero dollar button.   
I will be making a few changes to the rules to make the game flow a little bit better. Those changes will be listed here, and may be changed as the game moves forward.

# Encumbrance

Encumbrance in the base game is a little bit of a hassle to track, for very little reward. Instead, I will be using a slot-based encumbrance system, akin to a lot of video games.  
Each character has a grid of slots that they place items on. Each row of six slots has a Strength score associated with it; placing items in rows that your Strength does not reach will encumber you. One extra row brings your movement to ¾ its base value; two extra rows halves your movement and prevents you from gaining bonuses to Surprise rolls; three extra rows brings your movement to ¼, prevents bonuses to Surprise, and incurs a \-1 penalty to Surprise; and four extra rows prevents you from moving, prevents bonuses to Surprise, and incurs a \-2 penalty to Surprise.  
Coins can stack up to fifty in a single slot, and small items like torches or rations can stack up to four. Large items (like a claymore or medium or larger shield) take up two adjacent slots. Very large or particularly unwieldy items, like a tent or cooking pot, take up three slots in a line. Certain small treasure items, like jewelry, do not take up slots at all (free money\!).   
Armor is a special case, and takes up a very large number of slots. Armor that reduces your movement to 90 ft (banded, padded, studded, ring mail, chain mail) takes up four slots in a 2x2 square. Armor that reduces your movement to 60 ft (splint, scale mail, plate mail) takes up nine slots in a 3x3 square. Armor that does not reduce your speed (leather, elfin mail) takes up one slot. Clothes that do not affect your armor class do not take up slots at all.   
Containers like backpacks or quivers do not take up slots; instead, they encompass a certain number of slots that other items can be placed in. You must have a container for an item unless it is currently being worn (like armor) or held (like a weapon or torch). Certain containers also have special effects on the slots they encompass. Details for each container are displayed in the table below.

| Container | Slots | Special Effects |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Backpack | Nine slots in a 3x3 square | None; all characters begin the game with a free backpack |
| Quiver | One or two adjacent slots, depending on quiver size | Ammunition that the quiver is designed for can be stacked up to 12\. |
| Flask | One | Liquids may be stacked up to 3 (pints of oil, vials of holy water, doses of deadly poison, etc.) |
| Writing Satchel | One | One book, ten sheets of parchment, and a set of writing tools may all be placed in the satchel together, despite not normally stacking. The parchment may be replaced with up to two scrolls. |
| Pouch/Purse | One or four in a 2x2 grid, depending on size. | None |
| Sack | Four in a 2x2 grid or nine in a 3x3 grid, depending on size.  | None |
| Scroll Case | One | Up to three scrolls can be stacked in this slot. |

# Character Creation

I’m making some minor changes to character creation to hopefully make things fun but deadly.   
**Ability Scores**: ~~To keep characters frail and killable~~, roll 3d6 for each score in order. After rolling, you may switch any two scores.  
**Ancestry**: I am removing the maximum levels from ancestries, so you don’t stop progressing with a character you enjoy. To make sure humans aren’t completely useless, they automatically get the maximum value on their first level hit points.  
Gnomes do not exist in this setting. Instead, the Imperial Goblin ancestry will use Gnome statistics, except that they learn an additional language instead of Gnomish. Anyone who would get Gnomish from their ancestry instead learns an additional language of their choice.  
Humans must select a culture that they are from, which gives them that culture’s language, in addition to their alignment tongue and another language of their choice. The available cultures are: Archontean, hailing from the Archontean empire that has recently begun to spread over this continent; Thorcin, a rural people that are native to the continent; or Wiskin, viking-like people from the northern lands.   
All characters may replace languages they start with with one of the additional languages from the setting: Archontean, Thorcin, or Wiskin from human cultures, or Mithric (ancient language primarily used by magic researchers). Common does not exist, and anyone who would learn common instead learns another language of their choice.  
**Class**: I am removing alignment restrictions from classes, although clerics still have to match the alignment of their deity.   
We will not be using multiclassing or dual classing in any characters, at least for a while. Since I reduced the drawbacks of demihumans, a multiclassed character could be way too strong, and dual classing is an awkward system.  
Magic Users also automatically know the Read Magic spell, in addition to the two random and two of their choice. Magic users must choose one of the Seven Collegia where they learned their magic, because other places of learning are outlawed.

# Experience and Levels

	There are some things in the leveling system that I’m not happy with, so I’m changing them\!  
In the base game, XP is gained in two main ways: A small amount for defeating monsters, and 1 XP per gold piece brought out of the dungeon to a safe location. There are some issues with this system; mostly, players are not rewarded for finding creative ways around monsters and for doing things without monetary reward (like exploration, the main impetus of the game). Additionally, players have to be constantly given more and more gold as they level up to make sure XP gain keeps up, destroying the economy of the world. In order to fix this, the game requires you to spend exorbitant amounts of gold on training, essentially removing the gold you had to gain for the XP. So, I am making some changes.  
**Gold no longer awards XP**. You will be able to spend gold for XP in certain ways (see below), but simply getting gold out of the dungeon no longer gives experience points.  
**Training is no longer required**. Once you have the XP required for your next level and get a rest in a safe location (i.e. not in the dungeon, or at least in a friendly faction’s base), you level up immediately.  
**There are XP rewards for exploration**. The details of this are subject to change as the game goes on, to make sure the rewards aren’t too fast or too slow, but you will be rewarded for discovery and creativity. Details will be below.

## Downtime and Training

	Since training no longer exists, you’ll notice a lot of gold suddenly lining your pockets. Don’t worry, there will be ways to spend it for significant benefits. In the table below, I will list standard downtime activities and their benefits; this is still a sandbox game, and if you have other plans, let me know and we can find a way to make it work.

| Activity | Cost | Benefit |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Training | Up to 1,000 GP per week | You train with an established NPC in town that has agreed to train you. Gain 1 XP per GP spent. |
| Connecting | Up to 1,000 GP per week | You spend time making connections in town. For good characters, this is frequently charity work, while neutral characters ingratiate themselves politically, although your character could break the mold. Gain 0.5 XP per GP spent. Your reputation in town improves the more time you spend Connecting. |
| Carousing | Up to 500 GP per week | You spend time relaxing, drinking, and making friends. Gain 1 XP per GP spent. Roll on the Carousing Effects table below. |
| Recuperating | Up to 500 GP per week | You spend time training lightly and maintaining your condition while recovering from injuries. All lasting effects (poison, injuries, lost HP) recover at double the rate. Gain 1 XP per GP spent. |

## Exploration Awards

	The XP gained from this will ensure that the reduced XP from spending gold on non-training expenses or low-XP downtime activities will not slow down leveling, and also incentivize the types of play patterns that the system is designed for. It will also increase XP gain as you level up, as a treat :).  
	Before each session, the Total XP (TXP) for the party will be calculated. This is the sum of total XP required for each player to advance from the start of their current level to the next one. For example, if the party had a level 1 fighter, thief, cleric, and magic user at the start of the session, the TXP for the session would be 7,150 XP, regardless of the current XP of any character (the fighter could be at 0 or 1400 XP, their TXP is still 1500). If the thief leveled up to 2 in the middle of the session, the TXP still would not change until the start of the next session.  
	Completing various exploration goals, called Tasks, award a certain percentage of the party’s TXP to all players, divided by the number of players. While henchmen and hirelings do not increase the TXP, they still get a half or quarter share of each reward respectively. Currently, XP for Task completion is awarded as soon as the Task is completed; if this slows down the session too much, I might change to awarding them at the end of the session.  
	There are three types of Task: minor, major, and extraordinary. Minor tasks are accomplished pretty easily, and represent smart thinking and planning; these include finding and disabling a trap without any injuries or discovering a well-hidden stash of treasure. Minor tasks award 2% of the current TXP. Major tasks represent rarer or more advanced challenges, and take some significant effort to get past, generally only encountered once or twice per season; this could include solving a difficult puzzle or completing a quest for a faction you’re trying to ally with. Major tasks award 5% of the current TXP. Extraordinary tasks represent very difficult tasks, multi-session goals and/or significant changes in the status quo; this could be taking down a faction or finally locating an artifact you’ve been searching for. Extraordinary tasks award 10% of the current TXP.
